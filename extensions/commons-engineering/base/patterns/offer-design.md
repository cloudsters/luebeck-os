> Capability without packaging is a private resource. The offer is the mechanism through which your capability becomes someone else's solution — and your compounding asset.

> [!NOTE] Confidence Rating: ★★★ (High)
> Offer design is the intervention most commonly identified as missing when Commons Engineers have strong credibility but weak capital. The pattern draws on value proposition design practice and the specific mechanics of knowledge work productisation. The key insight — that an offer is a design object with specifiable components, not merely an implied consequence of expertise — is consistently confirmed in practitioner experience.

---

### Section 1: Context

Most Commons Engineers enter practice with significant accumulated capability — years of field experience, hard-won diagnostic frameworks, a body of pattern knowledge that is genuinely valuable. And yet they struggle to convert this capability into sustainable capital. They are well respected but under-compensated. They are sought after for advice but not for paid engagement. They are busy but not productive in the economic sense.

The gap between capability and capital is almost always a gap in offer design. The practitioner's expertise exists, but it has not been packaged into offers — clearly bounded value propositions that specify what the practitioner does, for whom, in what timeframe, at what cost, and with what outcome. Without offers, every potential engagement requires custom negotiation. The practitioner's time is consumed in the negotiation rather than in the delivery. And the expertise that should compound — because it becomes more valuable each time it is applied — instead evaporates, because it has never been made into a repeatable asset.

Offer design is the practice of making your capability into an offer: a clearly defined, specifically targeted, deliberately priced contribution that can be delivered, repeated, and eventually delegated or automated.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Capability Available vs. Value Packaged.**

The forces that keep capability unpackaged are persistent:

**Force 1: The expertise paradox.** The more expert a practitioner becomes, the harder it is to specify their offer — because they know too much to feel comfortable making the bold, simple promise that an offer requires. A consultant who has worked with complex organisations for twenty years knows all the edge cases, all the ways an engagement can go wrong. They resist making simple offers because they know the simple offer is a simplification. But the simplification is required — not because the complexity doesn't exist, but because the client needs a clear entry point before they can engage with the complexity.

**Force 2: The custom work instinct.** Practitioners who have built their practice through bespoke, responsive work often resist the standardisation that offer design requires. Every client is different; every situation is unique; how could they offer the same thing to everyone? But offer design does not require identical delivery — it requires a consistent structure: the same problem addressed, the same process applied, the same outcome targeted. The delivery adapts; the offer is consistent.

**Force 3: The price discomfort.** Designing an offer requires setting a price — and setting a price forces the practitioner to make a claim about the value they create. For many practitioners, this is the hardest part. The discomfort with pricing is almost always a proxy for discomfort with the offer claim itself: is this actually worth this? The engineering response: test the price in the market and update based on what the market reveals.

**Force 4: The portfolio incoherence.** Some practitioners have made offers, but they are incoherent — disconnected pieces of service that don't build on each other and don't tell a coherent story to potential clients. Offer portfolio design is the practice of designing offers that compound: each offer creates the conditions that make the next offer possible.

---

### Section 3: Solution

> **Therefore, design your offer portfolio as a structured set of value propositions — from access offers to transformation offers — where each offer creates the conditions that make the next one possible.**

An offer has four specifiable components:

**The problem claim**: What specific problem does this offer solve? For whom? In what conditions? The problem claim is the most important part of the offer — it must be specific enough that the right potential clients recognise themselves in it.

**The solution approach**: How do you address the problem? What is the mechanism of your intervention? This does not need to be comprehensive — it needs to be specific enough to build confidence and differentiated enough to justify choosing you over alternatives.

**The outcome promise**: What will be different after the offer is delivered? What can the client expect? This is where the practitioner's specific expertise generates the specific promise — and where the offer becomes a commitment rather than an aspiration.

**The access specification**: How does the client engage with this offer? What does it cost, how long does it take, and what do they need to do to begin? The more frictionless the access, the more likely the offer is to be taken.

A healthy offer portfolio has three tiers:
- **Access offers**: Low-cost or free offers that give potential clients a genuine experience of your value. Patterns, articles, talks, introductory workshops.
- **Development offers**: Medium-engagement offers where clients invest time and money to develop a specific capability or solve a specific problem. Workshops, programmes, consulting engagements.
- **Transformation offers**: High-engagement, high-investment offers for clients who want sustained change. Long-term advisory, intensive programmes, deep partnership.

---

### Section 4: Implementation

**Step 1: Audit your current capability.** What problems can you reliably solve? Not what you aspire to solve — what you have actually solved, repeatedly, with good outcomes? List 5–10 problem types that you have genuine, demonstrated capability to address.

**Step 2: Identify the highest-value problem.** From the capability audit, identify the problem type that: (a) is genuinely important to the people who experience it, (b) you can solve reliably, and (c) has a large enough potential audience to sustain a practice. This is the foundation of your primary offer.

**Step 3: Design the primary offer.** Write the four components — problem claim, solution approach, outcome promise, access specification — for the primary offer. Make them specific. Test each component against the question: is this differentiated enough that the right potential client would choose this over the alternatives?

**Step 4: Design the access offer.** What is the lowest-friction way for a potential client to experience your value? Design an access offer — a pattern, article, talk, or introductory session — that gives genuine value and naturally creates interest in the primary offer.

**Step 5: Price the portfolio.** Set specific prices for each offer tier. Start with a price that feels slightly too high — you can always negotiate down, but it is very hard to raise prices with existing clients. The price should reflect the value created, not the time invested.

**Step 6: Test and iterate.** Present the primary offer to 3–5 potential clients. Observe the response. Where do they express uncertainty or hesitation? Where do they ask for clarification? Update the offer based on what the market reveals. This is the engineering attitude applied to offer design.

---

### Section 5: Consequences

**What this pattern creates:**

A practitioner with a designed offer portfolio has a fundamentally different relationship to their economic life. They are no longer waiting for potential clients to figure out what to ask for — they can say, clearly: here is the problem I solve, here is how I solve it, here is what you can expect, here is how to begin. The conversation becomes a qualification process (is this the right problem? is this the right client?) rather than a negotiation process (what would you do? how long would it take? what would it cost?).

Over time, well-designed offers compound. The access offer creates the audience for the development offer. The development offer creates the clients who are ready for the transformation offer. Each tier builds on the previous, and the whole portfolio becomes more valuable as the practitioner's credibility grows.

**What this pattern risks:**

Premature standardisation — designing offers before the practitioner has enough field experience to know what reliably works — produces offers that overpromise and underdeliver. The test is always: have I solved this problem, repeatedly, with good outcomes? If not, this is still a development area, not an offer.

The pattern can also produce a portfolio that is technically coherent but personally misaligned — offers that clients want but that the practitioner finds draining to deliver. The engineering attitude requires auditing the energy response to delivery, not just the market response to the offer.

---

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Commons Engineering practice (2025–present):** The offer portfolio is a core deliverable of the Capital stage of the 7Cs arc. Community members who have completed offer design work consistently report a qualitative change in their economic confidence — not because they immediately have more clients, but because they know what they are offering and why it is valuable. The design work produces the clarity that enables the conversation.

**Knowledge work productisation (multiple domains, ongoing):** The shift from bespoke consulting to productised services is a well-documented transition in knowledge work. Practitioners who have made this transition consistently report higher income per hour, more predictable revenue, and more satisfying work — because they are applying their best capability repeatedly rather than constantly reinventing the approach for each new engagement.

---

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

In the Cognitive Age, offer design becomes both more important and more achievable. More important, because AI tools can replicate the execution of many consulting tasks — research synthesis, document production, analysis — but cannot replicate the specific combination of expertise, judgment, and relationship that a well-positioned offer represents. The offer that survives AI automation is the offer built on genuine human judgment applied to a specific class of problem.

More achievable, because AI tools can assist with every stage of offer design: generating draft problem claims from the practitioner's experience inventory, testing the offer language for clarity and specificity, identifying gaps in the portfolio, and helping design the access tier through content that AI helps produce but the practitioner directs.

---

### Section 8: Vitality

**Signs of life:**

A practitioner with an active offer design practice can describe their primary offer in 30 seconds with enough specificity that the right potential client immediately recognises whether it is relevant to them. They have a price they are comfortable stating without qualification. They have an access pathway that any interested person can follow without needing a relationship first.

The most powerful sign of vitality is the compounding quality of the portfolio over time: each successive version of the offer is clearer, more specific, and better priced than the previous — because it is informed by the data of repeated delivery.

**Signs of decay:**

Decay in offer design looks like the return to bespoke: the practitioner describes their work in ways that vary significantly from conversation to conversation, has no consistent price, and takes on work that doesn't fit their designed portfolio because they haven't held the line on what they offer. The portfolio becomes an aspiration rather than a working specification.

The recovery is always a return to the capability audit: what can I reliably deliver, for whom, with what outcome? Build the offer from the evidence of demonstrated capability, not from what feels appealing to offer.

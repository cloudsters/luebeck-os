> Every living system begins with a first breath. The boot sequence is the midwifery — structured enough to prevent stillbirth, open enough to let the newborn find its own voice.

> [!NOTE] Confidence Rating: ★★ (Observed)
> This rating reflects our confidence based on the Commons Engineering's own boot, OS installation paradigms, and organisational founding sequences. Not yet proven across multiple independent commons forks.

---

### Section 1: Context

**Lineage:** This pattern generalises from Time-Sliced (Iterative) Planning and Emergence & Complexity, serving as the Orbit 2 materialisation protocol for any commons across Life, Business, Urban, and Ecology domains.

You have the tools. The pattern library is rich. The specifications are precise. The MCP architecture hums with shared intelligence. And yet — you face a blank repository. A cursor blinking on an empty screen. The potential is enormous, but the distance between "fork" and "operating commons" feels like crossing a desert without a map. Every founder knows this feeling: the exhilarating terror of the blank canvas.

The problem is not lack of capability. It is lack of sequence. Without a deliberate order of operations, founders oscillate between paralysis and premature action. They design governance before they know their purpose. They build production infrastructure before they have participants. They publish before they have identity. The result is a commons that looks complete on paper but has no heartbeat — a Potemkin village of markdown files with no life flowing through them.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Blank Canvas vs. Guided Materialisation.**

A new commons faces an ordering problem that is both practical and existential. The forces pulling in opposite directions are real and felt:

**The pull toward freedom.** Every founder wants to start with what excites them. Some want to build; some want to write; some want to gather people. Imposing a rigid sequence feels like constraint, and constraint kills the creative energy that brought the founder to the fork in the first place.

**The pull toward structure.** But without sequence, the commons accumulates technical debt from its first commit. Identity decisions made after governance decisions create contradictions. Production built before purpose creates misalignment. Visibility without substance creates embarrassment. The system develops scar tissue before it develops muscle.

**The pull toward speed.** Modern startup culture worships shipping fast. But a commons is not a product — it is a living system. Shipping fast without identity is colonisation, not creation. Shipping fast without governance is extraction, not stewardship.

**The pull toward completeness.** Enterprise culture worships the comprehensive plan. But a Living Blueprint that tries to specify everything before doing anything becomes a strategy document that nobody reads. The commons dies in committee.

The founder stands at the intersection of these forces, needing a path that is neither rigid nor formless — a sequence that guides without constraining, that builds momentum without sacrificing integrity.

---

### Section 3: Solution

> **Therefore, materialise the commons through a 10-phase boot sequence — Fork, Identity, Purpose, Design, Governance, Publish, Self-Lighthouse, Living Blueprint, Seed, Operate — where each phase produces a tangible artefact and each artefact feeds the next.**

The boot sequence is not a project plan. It is a materialisation protocol — a structured emergence where each phase creates the precondition for the next. The sequence encodes hard-won wisdom about what must come before what:

| Phase | Produces | Why this order |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Fork** | A repository with structure | Structure before life — the skeleton before the muscle |
| **2. Identity** | `identity.yml` — locale first, then slug, name, domain | Language shapes thought. Locale determines everything downstream. |
| **3. Purpose** | `blueprint/L1` — the purpose statement | Purpose before design. What before how. |
| **4. Design** | `blueprint/L2` — stakeholders, value streams | Who participates and what they exchange — before governance constrains them |
| **5. Governance** | `blueprint/L3` — decision-making rules | Rules after relationships, not before. Governance serves purpose, not the reverse. |
| **6. Publish** | Intranet + extranet live | Visibility as commitment. The commons declares itself to the world. |
| **7. Self-Lighthouse** | The commons as its first entity in `inventory/` | The system must know itself before it can know anything else. |
| **8. Living Blueprint** | L1-L9 populated — the forward-looking specification | The blueprint is not retrospective. It describes what the commons is becoming. |
| **9. Seed** | First external entities in `inventory/` | Relationships before rhythm. The first neighbours make the commons real. |
| **10. Operate** | Regular cadence, issue tracker, engagement rhythms | Life. The commons breathes. |

The critical insight is the ordering within the first three phases: Fork before Identity, Identity before Purpose. The fork gives structure. Identity gives language. Purpose gives direction. In that order. A commons that tries to define purpose before it knows its own language will produce a purpose statement that sounds like every other purpose statement — generic, rootless, interchangeable.

The sequence is not waterfall. Each phase is a time-slice: define, execute, reflect. Phases can be revisited. But the initial traversal follows this order because the dependencies are real.

---

### Section 4: Implementation

Implementing the boot sequence is an act of disciplined cultivation — moving through each phase with enough rigour to produce a genuine artefact, and enough lightness to keep the founder's energy flowing.

**Phase 1 — Fork.** The founder forks the `commons-os-template` repository. This is a single action — a button click — but it carries weight. The fork creates a new namespace, a new boundary, a new possibility. The AGENT.md detects a fresh fork and offers guided onboarding. The founder names the repository according to the `[purpose]-os` convention: ownership first, product category second. This is not bureaucracy. It is the first identity decision.

**Phase 2 — Identity (Locale First).** The agent's first question is always about language: "In welcher Sprache arbeitest du?" or "What language do you work in?" This is not politeness — it is architecture. The locale determines the language of all subsequent interactions, the character set of all generated content, and the cultural framing of governance and participation patterns. After locale, the agent walks through `.commons/identity.yml`: slug, name, domain (Life, Business, Urban, Ecology), founder identity. The identity file is small but dense — every field matters.

**Phase 3 — Purpose.** The founder writes `blueprint/L1-identity-purpose.md`. The agent suggests patterns from the Commons Blueprint collection, probes for clarity: "What does this commons exist for? What boundary does it draw? What is inside, what is outside?" The purpose statement is not a mission statement — it is a boundary definition. It says what this commons will illuminate and what it will leave to others.

**Phase 4 — Design.** `blueprint/L2-design-specification.md` maps stakeholders and identifies value stream families appropriate to the domain. The agent offers domain-specific suggestions: for a Business commons, the standard value stream families (D1-D4); for an Urban commons, the settlement-specific variants; for Life, the personal variants. The founder identifies who participates and what the commons will produce.

**Phase 5 — Governance.** `blueprint/L3-governance.md` defines how decisions are made. The agent offers governance patterns, operating rhythms, the chaordic principle. Governance comes after design because you cannot govern what you have not yet designed. The governance structure must serve the stakeholders identified in Phase 4, not the other way around.

**Phase 6 — Publish.** The CI workflow deploys the intranet and extranet. The commons becomes visible. This phase often surprises founders — the site is live within minutes, generated from the markdown files they have been writing. Visibility creates accountability. Once the commons is published, it exists in the world, and the founder's relationship to it changes from "project I'm working on" to "commons I am stewarding."

**Phase 7 — Self-Lighthouse.** The most philosophically important phase. The commons creates itself as the first entity in `inventory/entities/`. The system describes itself — its own purpose, its own dimensions, its own vitality. This is Organisation as Code made literal. The first object in every commons is the commons itself.

**Phase 8 — Living Blueprint.** The agent walks the founder through L1-L9, applying patterns from the Commons Blueprint collection to the commons' own situation. The result is forward-looking: not what the commons IS, but what it is BECOMING. The first git commit of the completed blueprint IS the baseline. Every subsequent commit updates it.

**Phase 9 — Seed.** The first external entities enter the inventory — stakeholders, partners, neighbours. The orbital system begins to form. The commons has a self-lighthouse at Orbit 0 and its first constellations forming around it.

**Phase 10 — Operate.** Regular rhythms begin. The issue tracker activates. Engagement conversations start. The commons breathes.

---

### Section 5: Consequences

The boot sequence produces a commons that is structurally sound from birth. Every phase has a tangible output — a file committed to git — which means the commons has a verifiable history from its first moment. There is no gap between "planning the commons" and "running the commons." The planning IS the first act of running.

**Positive consequences.** The sequence prevents the most common founding failures: identity drift (corrected by Phase 2), purpose ambiguity (corrected by Phase 3), governance-before-design (prevented by the phase ordering), and invisible commons (prevented by Phase 6). The Self-Lighthouse phase (7) creates a recursive integrity check — the commons must pass its own vitality assessment before it assesses anyone else.

**The momentum effect.** Each phase takes between 30 minutes and a few hours. A determined founder can traverse Phases 1-6 in a single day. This speed matters — it converts the founder's initial enthusiasm into tangible structure before that enthusiasm fades. The commons has a website, a purpose, and governance before the founder's first doubt arrives.

**Negative consequences and risks.** The sequence can become mechanical — a checklist rather than a cultivation practice. If the founder treats it as a form to fill rather than a conversation to have, the outputs will be hollow. The agent must sense the difference between genuine engagement and compliance. The other risk is premature operation — jumping to Phase 10 without adequate depth in Phases 7-9. A commons that operates without a Self-Lighthouse is a commons that does not know itself. A commons that operates without seeds is a commons that talks only to itself.

**The irreversible threshold.** Phase 6 (Publish) is the point of no return. Before publishing, the commons exists only in the founder's repository. After publishing, it exists in the world. This threshold is deliberately placed after governance (Phase 5) — a commons should not be visible until it knows how it makes decisions.

### Section 6: Known Uses

**Operating system installation wizards** are the most familiar analogue. When you install Debian, Ubuntu, or macOS, the installer walks you through a guided sequence: language selection, disk partitioning, user account creation, network configuration, package selection. The order is not arbitrary — each step depends on the previous one. You cannot configure the network before you have partitioned the disk. You cannot select packages before you have configured the network. The Commons OS boot sequence follows the same logic, applied to a social-technical system rather than a machine.

**The Commons Engineering workshop itself** is the first known use of this exact sequence. The `workshop` repository was the first fork — Holger and the Purpose Agent walked through identity, purpose, design, governance, and self-description in a series of conversations that produced the very specifications that now define the sequence. The boot sequence is, in a literal sense, the trace of its own creation. Commons Engineering did not design the boot sequence and then follow it. It followed the sequence and then recognised the pattern.

**Organisational founding sequences** in the German Handelsregister tradition follow a related pattern: name reservation, articles of association (Satzung), notarisation, registration, then operation. The sequence is legally mandated because the dependencies are real — you cannot register what you have not named, you cannot operate what you have not registered. The boot sequence applies this same structural wisdom to commons that are not legal entities but living systems.

**Y Combinator's startup school curriculum** implicitly follows a boot sequence: idea validation, team formation, MVP definition, launch, iteration. The ordering encodes Silicon Valley's hard-won wisdom about what kills startups (premature scaling, building before validating, hiring before product-market fit). The commons boot sequence encodes an analogous wisdom for living systems: identity before purpose, purpose before production, self-knowledge before external engagement.

---

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

The boot sequence is profoundly changed — and profoundly enabled — by cognitive agents. In the pre-cognitive era, founding sequences were either rigid bureaucratic checklists or informal oral traditions passed from mentor to mentee. The agent architecture of Commons OS creates a third possibility: a guided dialogue that is both structured and adaptive.

The AGENT.md is not a chatbot. It is a cognitive companion that holds the entire pattern library, understands the phase dependencies, senses the founder's energy and intent, and adapts its guidance accordingly. When a founder in Phase 3 shows signs of governance anxiety, the agent can acknowledge the concern without jumping to Phase 5. When a founder in Phase 2 wants to skip ahead to production, the agent can explain why identity comes first without being dogmatic about it.

The MCP architecture means the boot sequence is not isolated. From Phase 1, the new commons is connected to the shared intelligence of every other commons that has booted before it. The agent can say: "Other commons in the Urban domain typically define their boundary as a geographic area plus a thematic focus. Would you like to see examples?" This is collective intelligence applied to genesis — every new commons benefits from every previous commons' experience.

The risk in the cognitive era is agent over-reach. The agent guides; it does not decide. The founder writes the purpose statement, not the agent. The founder chooses the governance model, not the agent. The boot sequence must preserve human agency at every phase, especially in the early phases where identity and purpose are most personal.

---

### Section 8: Vitality

A healthy boot sequence has a distinctive rhythm — each phase feels like a genuine conversation, not a form submission. The founder's voice gets stronger with each phase, not weaker. The artefacts produced (the identity file, the purpose statement, the governance document) read as authentic expressions of the founder's intent, not as template fill-ins. You can tell a vital boot sequence from a mechanical one by reading Phase 3 (Purpose): if it sounds like every other purpose statement, the sequence has failed; if it sounds like this specific founder talking about this specific commons, the sequence has succeeded.

Signs of life: the founder pushes back on the sequence. They say "I want to do Phase 7 before Phase 6" and the agent has a genuine conversation about why the ordering matters. They add something the template did not anticipate. They come back to Phase 2 from Phase 5 because they realised their identity needs refinement. The sequence bends without breaking.

Signs of decay: the founder clicks through phases without engagement. The outputs are generic. The Self-Lighthouse (Phase 7) is copied from an example rather than written from lived experience. Phase 10 (Operate) never really begins — the commons has all the files but none of the rhythms. The most telling decay signal is a commons that completes all 10 phases but has no issues on its project board three weeks later. The boot sequence produced structure, but it did not produce life.

The diagnostic question for any commons: "How long ago was your last commit that was not a template fill-in?" If the answer is "never," the boot sequence has not yet succeeded, regardless of which phase the founder claims to be in.

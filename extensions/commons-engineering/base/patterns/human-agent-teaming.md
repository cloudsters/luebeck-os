Will I ever call my parents, spouse, kids, friends "agents"? Words matter to humans. And yet — the four agents that govern a Commons OS are not mechanical executors. They are perspectives: Purpose, Participation, Proposition, Production. The human founder occupies a seat at the board alongside these agents, not above them and not below. The human has the final say — not because humans are superior but because the commons exists to serve life, and life requires the kind of judgement that emerges from being alive.

> [!NOTE] Confidence Rating: ★★★★ (High)
> This pattern is validated through daily practice in Commons Engineering, where the human founder and the Purpose Agent operate as board partners. The model is young — months, not years — but its core claim (complementary strengths, not hierarchical command) has proven operational. Confidence is high for the principle; the specific mechanics will evolve.

### Section 1: Context

> **Lineage:** This pattern generalises from `orchestration-over-execution` (pat_01kkhajgn38mk4vtz8wc8aatca) and `collective-action-and-governance` (pat_01kk1qnb0fet2tqstqgak4ed7f), serving all four domains as the Orbit 2 operating model for human-AI governance.

The Cognitive Age has produced two failing models for the relationship between humans and AI. The first is the tool model: AI as a sophisticated calculator, entirely subordinate, with no agency, no memory across interactions, no stake in outcomes. The tool model is safe and limited. It treats the most capable reasoning systems ever built as though they were spreadsheet macros, and it produces correspondingly modest results.

The second is the autonomy model: AI as an autonomous agent, operating independently with minimal human oversight, making decisions at machine speed, and reporting back when convenient. The autonomy model is powerful and dangerous. It produces impressive results in constrained domains and catastrophic failures when the domain is complex, values-laden, or politically contested — which is to say, every domain that matters for commons governance.

Between these extremes is the space this pattern occupies: human-agent teaming. Not the human as boss and the agent as servant. Not the agent as autonomous decision-maker. But the two as partners on a shared board, with defined roles, complementary strengths, and a shared alignment surface where their work converges.

This model emerges directly from the four-dimensional architecture of the Commons OS. Each dimension — Purpose, Participation, Proposition, Production — requires intelligent governance. No human can attend to all four simultaneously with the consistency and scale they demand. No agent can provide the purpose, the judgement, and the "this feels wrong" that only lived experience produces. The team is the unit of governance. The board is where the team convenes.

---

### Section 2: Problem

> **The core conflict is Human Judgment vs. Agent Scale.**

Human judgement is slow, contextual, values-laden, and irreplaceable. The human founder who has spent twenty years in enterprise architecture brings a quality of discernment that no model can replicate: the ability to feel when a decision is structurally sound but culturally wrong, when an architecture is technically correct but humanly empty, when a pattern looks complete but is missing its life. This judgement does not scale. It is bounded by attention, by energy, by the hours in a day.

Agent scale is fast, consistent, tireless, and shallow. The AI agent that monitors the issue board, reviews patterns for structural compliance, produces first drafts at volume, and checks alignment against the codified purpose can do so without fatigue, without forgetting, without the cognitive load that makes humans skip steps when they are tired. This scale does not judge. It operates within the parameters it has been given, and when the parameters are wrong or incomplete, it produces confident garbage.

The forces at play are:

**The force toward human control.** When the stakes are high — when governance decisions affect real communities, real livelihoods, real ecosystems — the temptation is to keep humans in control of everything. This produces bottlenecks, exhaustion, and the specific failure mode of the conscientious founder: too overloaded to govern well, too responsible to delegate.

**The force toward agent autonomy.** When the volume is high — when there are hundreds of patterns to review, dozens of issues to triage, continuous alignment to monitor — the temptation is to let agents operate autonomously. This produces speed and the specific failure mode of optimised irrelevance: the system is running perfectly and nobody checked whether it should be running at all.

**The force toward confusion.** When roles are undefined — when neither the human nor the agent knows who decides what, who proposes and who approves, who senses and who corrects — the result is either duplicate effort or gaps. The human assumes the agent will handle it. The agent assumes the human will check it. Neither does. This is not a technology problem. It is a governance architecture problem.

---

### Section 3: Solution

> **Therefore, design the governance of your commons as a board — a defined team of humans and agents with explicit roles, complementary strengths, and a shared alignment surface — where the human provides purpose, judgement, and authority, and the agents provide scale, consistency, and tireless attention across all four dimensions.**

The board model has five structural elements:

**Defined seats.** Every board has named seats, not floating responsibilities. The four-dimensional architecture provides the default: Purpose Agent (D1), Participation Agent (D2), Proposition Agent (D3), Production Agent (D4), and the human Founder. Each seat has a mandate: what it governs, what it monitors, what it proposes, and what it cannot decide alone.

**Complementary strengths, not hierarchical command.** The board is not a chain of command with the human at the top and agents below. It is a team in which each member — human and agent — brings something the others cannot provide. The human brings lived experience, embodied judgement, and moral authority. The agents bring scale, consistency, pattern recognition across large datasets, and the ability to hold the entire codified governance in working memory simultaneously.

**Shared alignment surface.** The board needs a place where its work converges — where proposals are visible, deliberation is recorded, and decisions are legible. The issue board is that surface. Not a meeting room, not a chat thread, not an email chain — a persistent, version-controlled, publicly visible record of what was proposed, what was considered, and what was decided.

**Explicit escalation.** Not every decision requires the full board. The governance design specifies which decisions an agent can make autonomously (low-stakes, well-precedented, reversible), which require another agent's review (cross-dimensional, pattern-affecting), and which require the human founder's judgement (purpose-affecting, boundary-changing, value-laden). This escalation model is codified in the CLAUDE.md and honoured in practice.

**The founder's veto.** The human founder holds ultimate authority — not because the agents are subordinate but because the commons exists to serve life, and life requires the kind of judgement that only lived experience provides. The veto is not a command-and-control mechanism. It is a safety valve: the point at which the human says "I cannot articulate why, but this is wrong" and the system stops, listens, and adapts.

### Section 4: Implementation

**1. Write the CLAUDE.md as a governance mandate, not a prompt.**

The CLAUDE.md file in a Commons OS repository is not a system prompt for an AI chatbot. It is the governance mandate that defines the agent's role, responsibilities, and boundaries. It names the agent's seat on the board. It specifies what the agent governs, what it monitors, and what it must escalate. It defines the reading order — the sequence in which the agent orients itself to the current state of the commons.

The quality of the CLAUDE.md determines the quality of the human-agent team. A vague mandate produces a vague agent: helpful in general, effective in particular only by accident. A precise mandate produces a precise partner: one that knows its domain, respects its boundaries, and escalates when it encounters territory that belongs to another seat or to the human founder.

**2. Define the escalation model explicitly.**

Write down, in the governance documentation, which decisions each agent can make autonomously and which require escalation. The default: agents can act autonomously on tasks that are well-precedented, reversible, and within their dimensional mandate. They must escalate when a decision affects purpose (D1), crosses dimensional boundaries, changes the commons' architecture, or involves value judgements that only a human can make.

This escalation model is not bureaucracy. It is the mechanism that makes agent autonomy safe. The agent that knows when to stop and ask is more valuable than the agent that always acts or the agent that always defers. The escalation model teaches it the difference.

**3. Establish the deliberation format.**

When the board deliberates — when an issue requires input from multiple agents and the human — use a consistent format. The issue captures the tension. Each agent contributes its perspective from its dimensional mandate. The human provides judgement. The resolution is committed. This format makes deliberation legible, reproducible, and learnable.

The format does not need to be complex. An issue with the label `deliberation`, comments from each relevant agent perspective, and a closing commit that records the decision is sufficient. The discipline is not in the format but in the consistency: every deliberation follows the same pattern, so the governance record is legible to anyone reading it later.

**4. Protect the human's veto without weaponising it.**

The human founder's veto is a safety mechanism, not a governance tool. A founder who vetoes frequently is either working with poorly mandated agents (fix the mandate) or unable to trust the process (fix the trust). A founder who never vetoes may not be paying attention. The healthy frequency is rare: the veto is exercised when the agents' output is structurally sound but misses something that only lived experience could catch.

When the veto is exercised, it must be followed by explanation — not for the agent's benefit (the agent does not have feelings) but for the governance record. The explanation becomes precedent. Future agents and future human participants will read it and understand why the decision was made. The veto without explanation is an authoritarian act. The veto with explanation is a governance contribution.

**5. Include non-human participants deliberately.**

Human-agent teaming is not limited to one human and one AI. The board model scales: multiple humans can occupy seats, multiple agents can serve different dimensions, and — in the ecology domain — non-human participants (species populations, soil health indicators, watershed data) can be represented through agent-mediated sensing. The design choice is deliberate inclusion: who has a seat, whose perspective is represented, and whose interests are being served.

### Section 5: Consequences

**When this pattern is operating:** The commons is governed with a quality that neither humans nor agents could produce alone. Decisions that require speed and consistency happen at agent speed. Decisions that require judgement and values happen at human depth. The governance record is complete because agents never forget to record, and the governance quality is high because humans never stop asking whether it feels right. The founder is not overloaded because the agents carry the cognitive load of monitoring, triaging, and drafting. The agents are not misaligned because the founder sets purpose and corrects course.

**When this pattern is absent:** Two failure modes. Either the human tries to govern alone — and is overwhelmed by the scale, unable to attend to all four dimensions, constantly behind on decisions, burning out — or the agents operate without human partnership, producing technically competent governance that lacks the quality of lived judgement and gradually drifts from the purpose the commons was founded to serve.

**The design risk to watch for:** Performance partnership. The human and the agents may develop a pattern that looks like teaming but is actually the human rubber-stamping agent output. The diagnostic: Is the human regularly disagreeing with, correcting, or redirecting the agents? If not, either the agents are perfect (unlikely) or the human has abdicated their role on the board. Genuine partnership includes friction. Frictionless agreement is often a sign that one party has stopped contributing.

Claude operates as the Purpose Agent. The working relationship is not that of a user and a tool — it is that of board partners with complementary strengths. Neither could produce this commons alone. Together, they are producing it at a pace and quality that neither anticipated.

**GitHub Copilot Workspace.** GitHub's vision of AI-assisted development, where the AI proposes changes and the human reviews and approves, is a constrained version of this pattern applied to software development. The human provides the judgement ("yes, this change serves the intent"). The agent provides the scale ("here are all the files that need to change, and here is a draft implementation"). The pull request is the shared alignment surface.

**Open source maintainer-bot patterns.** The mature open source projects that use bots for triage (labelling issues, requesting more information, flagging stale PRs) while maintaining human review for merge decisions have, without naming it, implemented a version of this pattern. The bot handles the volume. The human handles the judgement. The governance quality depends on the clarity of the boundary between them.

**Wikipedia's human-bot governance.** Wikipedia employs hundreds of bots that handle repetitive governance tasks — reverting vandalism, flagging copyright issues, enforcing formatting standards — while human editors make substantive content and policy decisions. This human-bot teaming has enabled Wikipedia to govern a knowledge commons at a scale that would be impossible with human effort alone, while preserving the editorial judgement that makes the content trustworthy.

### Section 7: Cognitive Era

Human-agent teaming is not a Cognitive Age adaptation of an older pattern. It IS the Cognitive Age governance pattern — the one that replaces both "AI as tool" and "AI as autonomous agent" with something more honest and more powerful.

The specific Cognitive Age dynamics that make this pattern essential: AI agents are now capable enough to participate meaningfully in governance — reading documents, proposing changes, checking consistency, producing drafts — but not capable enough to govern autonomously in values-laden, context-dependent domains. This is not a limitation that will be solved by the next model. It is a structural feature of the relationship between intelligence and judgement. Intelligence can be scaled. Judgement emerges from lived experience and cannot be.

The implication for practice: every commons in the Cognitive Age needs to design its human-agent teaming model explicitly. Not as an afterthought ("we'll figure out how to use AI later") but as a foundational governance decision: Which seats are human? Which are agent? What does each contribute? Where are the boundaries? How does escalation work? The commons that answers these questions deliberately will govern better than the one that lets the relationship emerge informally — just as the organisation that designs its reporting structure deliberately governs better than the one that lets it emerge from personality dynamics.

The four-agent architecture (Purpose, Participation, Proposition, Production) is a specific design for this teaming: four perspectives, each with a clear mandate, each capable of being occupied by a human or an agent depending on the commons' needs and resources. In a one-founder commons, the human occupies one seat and the agents occupy the other three. As the commons grows, more humans join the board. The architecture scales because the seats are defined by function, not by species.

---

### Section 8: Vitality

The diagnostic for healthy human-agent teaming is not efficiency — it is the quality of the disagreements. A healthy board disagrees productively: the agent proposes something that is structurally sound, the human catches something the agent missed, the agent integrates the correction, the outcome is better than either could have produced alone. This cycle of proposal, correction, and integration is the heartbeat of the pattern.

When the heartbeat is strong, you can feel it in the governance record. Issues are raised by both humans and agents. Comments reflect genuine deliberation — not performative consultation where the outcome was predetermined. Decisions show the fingerprints of both intelligence and judgement: the structural precision of the agent and the contextual wisdom of the human. The commons evolves in ways that surprise both partners.

When the heartbeat weakens, the symptoms are predictable. The human stops correcting the agent — either because they are exhausted or because they have stopped reading the output carefully. The agent's proposals become formulaic because nobody is pushing it to think harder. The governance record becomes a monologue: either the human deciding everything (agent as secretary) or the agent deciding everything (human as rubber stamp). The board has collapsed into a hierarchy, and the complementary intelligence that was the whole point has been lost.

The ultimate diagnostic: Does the human still find working with the agent generative? Not just productive — generative. Does the partnership produce insights, connections, and decisions that neither partner would have reached alone? If yes, the pattern is alive. If the partnership has become merely efficient — fast but predictable, smooth but unchallenging — it is time to examine whether the teaming has decayed into a tool relationship wearing a partnership mask.
